    3D Organ Bioprinting: The Future of Organ Transplants and Accessibility

    Ava Narag

    Researchers at Wake Forest have been growing organs and tissues in the lab for years, using 3D printing to create mini-kidneys and mini-livers. The challenge now is making larger, fully functional organs, which they haven't quite achieved at organ scale.

    They've successfully 3D printed flat structures like skin, tubular structures like blood vessels, and hollow non-tubular organs like bladders. But bigger solid organs are tricky due to issues like vascularity, nutrition, and cell quality.

    Quality is a concern; for instance, heart cells from stem cells don't beat as strongly as natural ones. Producing enough cells for an organ is resource-intensive, and integrating these tissues into the body with blood vessels, nerves, and different cell types is complex. Still, progress is visible, like 3D-printed heart cells on a chip for drug testing.

    In the short term, 3D printing offers scale, precision, lower costs, reproducibility, and reduced organ rejection risk. While full-sized 3D-printed organ transplants are likely 20 to 30 years away, many remain optimistic that printed organs could become a reality within two decades. It's not science fiction; it's science in action.

    Examples of Artificial Organs and How They Work

    Artificial sensory organs

    Researchers have long sought ways to treat sensory dysfunctions and nervous system injuries. Traditional methods like nerve transplantation have limitations because they rely on slow nerve regeneration for recovery.

    Artificial sensory organs offer a faster solution by directly transmitting information to the brain. These devices come in various types, from helping with vision and hearing to taste and touch, all by stimulating nerves. Recent advances include things like artificial retinas and cochlear implants, while scientists are also working on innovative artificial nerves that can compensate for spinal cord damage by connecting with the brain and muscles.

    Artificial kidney and artificial liver

    There are many attempts to develop artificial systems for treating kidney and liver diseases, but they come with their challenges. Traditional methods like dialysis machines and bioartificial supports can limit patients' activities, require strict adherence to diets and medications, and can be costly.

    One approach involves renal assistance devices (RADs), which try to mimic kidney functions using a combination of filters and bioreactors. Researchers are working on making these devices smaller and implantable. They're also exploring various materials like pig kidneys, discarded human kidneys, or synthetic polymers to create scaffolds for bioartificial kidneys.

    Similarly, artificial livers could help with organ shortages in treating liver failure. Scientists have even created something called "liver-buds" using stem cells to rescue animals with liver failure. It's all about finding innovative ways to tackle these health challenges.

    Artificial heart

    Scientists and doctors use 3D printing to plan heart surgeries and make custom-fit implants. They've come up with clever ways to boost implant success, like using special materials to create cardiac-specific structures. In experiments, they've even crafted intricate heart components, such as branched coronary arteries and embryonic hearts, using hydrogels and data from CT and MRI scans. It's like creating detailed 3D models of the heart to help improve surgical procedures.

    First Case of Bioprinting

    Last year in San Antonio, Texas, Dr. Arturo Bonilla performed a groundbreaking procedure: he implanted a 3D-bioprinted ear made from the patient's own cartilage cells. While Dr. Bonilla is a seasoned pediatric microtia surgeon, this case was unique, marking the convergence of science fiction with reality.

    3D bioprinting is making significant strides in the field of medical research and practice. Although the progress is gradual, it's undeniably real. According to Professor Tal Dvir, a leading expert in tissue engineering, he envisions a future where we can 3D-print organs for transplantation within a decade, starting with simpler tissues like skin and cartilage and eventually progressing to complex organs like the heart, liver, and kidney.
